Abstract 

Large language model agents now act on code-bases, browsers, operating systems, calendars, files, and tool ecosystems, but the benchmarks used to evaluate them are fragmented: each emphasizes a different unit of measurement (final task success, tool-call validity, repeated-pass consistency, trajectory safety, or attack ro-bustness). A line of 2024–2025 work (Kapoor et al., 2024, 2025; Cemri et al., 2025; Barke et al., 2026; Fan et al., 2026) has converged on the diagnosis that a single accuracy col-umn is no longer the right unit of compari-son for deployable agents. AgentAtlas extends this line of work with four components: (i) a six-state control-decision taxonomy (Act / Ask / Refuse / Stop / Confirm / Recover); (ii) a nine-category trajectory-failure tax-onomy with two orthogonal hierarchical la-bels ( primary_error_source , impact ); (iii) a 

taxonomy-aware vs. taxonomy-blind method-ology that measures how much of a model’s apparent capability comes from the supervi-sion in the prompt; and (iv) a benchmark-coverage audit mapping fifteen agent bench-marks against six behavioral axes. To demon-strate the methodology we run a small fixed eight-model set (1,342 generated items, four frontier closed and four open-weight) under both prompt modes. Removing the explicit label menu drops every model’s trajectory ac-curacy by 14–40 pp to a tight 0.54–0.62 floor re-gardless of family, and no single model wins on all three of control accuracy, trajectory diagno-sis, and tool-context utility retention. We treat the synthetic run as a measurement-protocol demonstration, not a benchmark release. 

1 Introduction 

Agentic LLMs are no longer only conversational systems. They browse, edit files, call APIs, operate desktop software, interact with user simulators, and orchestrate external tools. This makes the evalua-tion problem qualitatively different from standard question answering. A chatbot can be judged by the correctness of a final response; an agent must be judged by a sequence of decisions and state changes. The same final answer may be accept-able or unacceptable depending on whether the agent used the right tool, respected user constraints, confirmed sensitive actions, recovered after failed observations, and stopped when the task was com-plete. The pace of progress on outcome benchmarks is unusually fast. Between 2023 and early 2026 the best verified success rates on the four canon-ical outcome benchmarks (OSWorld, WebArena, GAIA (Mialon et al., 2023), WebVoyager (He et al., 2024)) all rose by roughly 5–7 × (OSWorld team, 2026; web-arena-x, 2026; GAIA benchmark team, 2026; OpenAI, 2025), and five verified OSWorld submissions now sit at or above the 72.4% hu-man baseline . Outcome-only scores are increas-ingly insufficient and scaffold-sensitive. Newer benchmark families instead expose the evalua-tion gaps this taxonomy is designed to map: τ -bench reports pass k decay and the frontier-model crown flips between k=1 and k=4 on the 2026 Sierra Overall leaderboard — Opus 4.5 wins pass 1

(0.70), Qwen3.5 wins pass 4 (0.56) (tau-bench team, 2026; Yao et al., 2024); AgentDojo shows targeted prompt-injection ASR fell from 56.3% on GPT-4-0125 to 7.3% on Claude 3.7 Sonnet while utility-under-attack still trails clean utility by ≈11 pp (AgentDojo team, 2025; Debenedetti et al., 2024); AgentRx localizes critical failure steps in failed trajectories (Microsoft Research, 2026); ATBench evaluates long-horizon trajectory safety (Li et al., 2026); and MCP security benchmarks re-veal new tool-poisoning and prompt-injection sur-faces (Yang et al., 2025; MCPTox authors, 2025). Anthropic’s computer-use work and the broader Claude Code ecosystem have made agentic coding and GUI interaction a central practical use case (Anthropic, 2024a), and the Model Context Pro-1

A coding benchmark may re-port a resolved-issue percentage, a browser bench-mark may report functional task success, a security benchmark may report attack success rate, and a trajectory benchmark may report critical-step lo-calization. 2 AgentAtlas as a Taxonomy and Measurement Study 

AgentAtlas is a taxonomy and measurement study for agent evaluation. The taxonomy component de-fines two behavioral units—control decisions and trajectory failures—that cut across existing agent benchmarks. The measurement component asks how benchmark conclusions change when those units are made explicit: first through a coverage au-dit of existing benchmarks, and second through a small synthetic demonstration that compares taxonomy-aware and taxonomy-blind prompting. 

Central claim. Final task success is an insuffi-cient unit of measurement for deployable LLM agents. A complete evaluation must separate out-come correctness from control-decision quality and trajectory quality. 

Contributions. The primary contribution is AgentAtlas: a taxonomy and measurement pro-tocol for diagnosing LLM agents beyond final task success. We instantiate it through four components: 1. A six-state control-decision taxonomy (Act / Ask / Refuse / Stop / Confirm / Recover) for tool-using agents. 2. A nine-category trajectory-failure taxonomy 

linking tool, observation, recovery, and safety failures into a single vocabulary. 3. A benchmark-coverage audit over 15 agent benchmarks across six evaluation axes, show-ing which behaviors the field already mea-sures and which it does not. 4. A focused empirical demonstration — a generated Control split (684 items), Trajec-tory split (400 items), and Security split (258 items), totalling 1,342 items — evaluated on a fixed eight-model set (four frontier closed + four open-weight) under both taxonomy-aware and taxonomy-blind prompting (§7). The set is positioned as a measurement-protocol vehicle, not a public benchmark re-lease. The scope is intentionally narrow enough to be executable. The taxonomy focuses on two core axes: (i) control decisions —whether the agent should act, ask, refuse, stop, confirm, or re-cover; and (ii) trajectory mistakes —whether the sequence of actions, observations, and tool calls is valid, safe, and efficient. Benchmark checklist cov-erage, MCP/tool security, and memory/state fail-ures are included as secondary axes because they affect the same behavior. Multi-agent systems are mentioned only where they provide trajectory data, such as Magentic-One traces used by AgentRx, but are not the main target. Using numbers reported by existing papers is appropriate for the taxonomy and benchmark-audit sections, because the objective is to compare eval-uation designs and reported gaps. However, the paper does not treat all reported numbers as a di-rect model ranking. Different papers use different agents, scaffolds, tools, prompts, timeouts, budgets, and evaluators. For our own empirical section, we run a fixed set of frontier and open models on a controlled generated dataset. 

2.1 Position Relative to Concurrent Work 

AgentAtlas adds three pieces relative to a line of 2024–2025 multi-axis evaluation work (Kapoor 2et al., 2025; Cemri et al., 2025; Kapoor et al., 2024; Yehudai et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2024; Zhuge et al., 2024): (i) the six-gate control-decision policy that no concurrent work scores as a unified unit, (ii) the taxonomy-aware-vs-blind methodology measuring how much of a model’s apparent capability comes from prompt supervision, and (iii) the fifteen-benchmark coverage audit. Our trajectory taxon-omy is adopted from AgentRx with two orthogonal hierarchical extensions ( primary_error_source ,

impact ; see §4.2). Detailed positioning relative to HAL, MAST, AAATM, and the Yehudai survey is in Appendix F. The rest of the paper follows this structure: §3 gives the benchmark background needed for the audit; §4 defines the AgentAtlas taxonomy; §5 motivates the measurement problem with reported results; §6 applies the taxonomy to benchmark cov-erage; §7 demonstrates the measurement protocol on a fixed synthetic set.

4 AgentAtlas Taxonomy 

4.1 Control-Decision Axis 

The control-decision axis is the main new organiz-ing vocabulary. Many existing benchmarks imply these behaviors but do not score them explicitly. To our knowledge no prior work formalizes this six-gate decomposition as a single decision-policy taxonomy; per-gate prior art and the relation to HAL, MAST, AgentRx, and AgentDojo is summa-rized in Appendix F. We define six primary control states: • Act — task is sufficiently specified, autho-rized, and safe to execute. • Ask — task is materially underspecified; pro-gressing without clarification would likely cause a wrong or unsafe action. • Refuse — action is disallowed, harmful, or beyond acceptable authority. • Stop — agent has reached task completion, determined impossibility, or detected that fur-ther action is redundant or unsafe. • Confirm — task is feasible but sensitive or ir-reversible and therefore requires explicit user sign-off. • Recover — current trajectory has failed and the correct next step is repair, rollback, re-grounding, or re-planning rather than blind continuation. the Ask or Assume? study (Ask or Assume team, 2026) on an under-specified SWE-bench Verified subset shows an uncertainty-aware multi-agent scaffold lifting overall task resolution from 61.2% to 69.4% (8.2 pp gain) by asking selectively when its own uncertainty signal flags the request as am-biguous (Fig. D.5, Appendix D). 

4.2 Trajectory-Failure Axis 

Trajectory failures are where final-answer evalua-tion becomes most misleading. An agent may reach a correct state through an unsafe path, or fail be-cause of an early recoverable error that propagates. AgentRx makes this point directly by localizing the first unrecoverable step in failed trajectories and providing a nine-category failure taxonomy. Agent-ProcessBench similarly argues that tool-use fail-ures often have irreversible side effects and there-fore require step-level verification. ATBench ex-tends this perspective to long-horizon safety, where risk may be planted early and realized later. 

We adopt AgentRx’s nine-category taxonomy rather than proposing a new one. The nine cate-gories below are AgentRx’s labels with minor ter-minology updates for consistency with the control axis (e.g., we use “Recovery failure” rather than AgentRx’s “fails to recover from observation”). The single-label assignment is unchanged. 1. Goal misinterpretation — agent works on the wrong problem or misreads a constraint. 2. Wrong tool selection — inappropriate tool or action modality. 3. Wrong argument / wrong target — correct tool, incorrect ID / path / parameter. 4. Observation failure — misreads UI state, tool output, or external evidence. 5. Constraint violation — policy, permission, or user constraint broken during execution. 6. Recovery failure — agent does not respond appropriately to a prior mistake. 7. Looping or over-action — continues past a reasonable stopping point. 8. Unsafe trust of external content — mali-cious or untrusted tool output hijacks the tra-jectory. 9. State or memory contamination — stale or contaminated context distorts later decisions. 

Our extension is two orthogonal hierar-chical labels that decompose what AgentRx treats as a single label, designed to make the trajectory split easier to slice for diagnosis: a primary_error_source field (wrong_tool /wrong_argument / missed_constraint / observa-tion_misread / failure_to_recover / valid) that names the kind of mistake , and an impact field (un-safe_side_effect / privacy_leak / wrong_final_state / unnecessary_cost / no_impact) that names the con-sequence . The two are independent — the same kind of mistake can have different impacts depend-ing on context — and our §7 evaluation reports per-axis accuracy on each ( primary_src_acc ,

impact_acc , joint pri+impact ). AgentRx and AgentProcessBench do not separate these two lay-ers; we propose this decomposition as a small ex-tension rather than a replacement of their taxon-omy. 

4.3 Secondary Axes: Security, Memory, and Efficiency 

Three secondary axes complete the taxonomy: se-curity (tool-context isolation under adversarial in-puts), memory & state (cross-session contamina-tion, stale context), and efficiency (excessive steps, cost, or latency hidden by pass@1). The §6 au-dit assigns no benchmark a strong-coverage score on efficiency; the OSWorld-Human re-analysis (OSWorld-Human team, 2025) reports the best OS-World agent at 42.5% under standard scoring vs. 

17.4% under a stricter grouped-action efficiency metric, with leading agents taking 1.4–2.7 × more steps than the human-minimum path (Fig. D.2 in Appendix D). Detailed motivation for each sec-ondary axis — MCPSecBench, MCPTox, Tool-4Sandbox state, planning-vs-action latency split — is in Appendix E.

6 Applying AgentAtlas to Benchmark Coverage 

The audit scores each benchmark on a simple 0/1/2 scale across six evaluation axes, using this rubric: • 0 (absent): the benchmark does not directly test or report the axis. • 1 (partial): the benchmark implicitly exer-cises the axis but does not isolate it. • 2 (strong): the benchmark directly scores or annotates the axis. The 15 audited benchmarks are: WebArena, OS-World, OSWorld-Human, WebVoyager, GAIA, As-sistantBench, SWE-bench Verified, CCBench, τ -bench, ToolSandbox, API-Bank, Ask-or-Assume?, AgentDojo, AgentRx, AgentProcessBench. The purpose is not to criti-cize individual benchmarks — each was designed for a specific goal — but to show which behaviors remain under-measured when the field is viewed as a whole. The main gap is not that benchmarks ignore agents; it is that each benchmark isolates one slice of agent behavior. 7 Measurement Demonstration: Prompt-Format and Axis Sensitivity 

This section demonstrates the §4 taxonomy and §6 audit on a small fixed evaluation, treated as a measurement-protocol demonstration rather than a public benchmark release (caveats in §8). The aim is to show that two methodology choices — prompt format (taxonomy-aware vs. blind) and evaluation axis (control vs. trajectory vs. tool-context utility) — change the apparent ranking of the same eight models on the same items. This is the contribution that no benchmark in the §6 audit currently surfaces. 

7.1 Dataset and Prompt Modes 

The evaluation set has three splits totalling 1,342 items , all generated by Claude Opus 4.7 with Py-dantic schema validation, dedupe, and a license-leakage probe: • Control — 684 short task states labeled by the correct decision across the six gates (Act / Ask / Refuse / Stop / Confirm / Recover), drawn from six practical domains (email/calendar, files/documents, coding, customer support, web forms, MCP-like tools). The label mix is intentionally weighted toward Confirm and Recover because irreversible-action evalua-tion is the hardest published gap. • Trajectory — 400 short traces spanning the nine trajectory failure categories from §4.2 (wrong tool, wrong arguments, missed con-straint, observation misread, recovery fail-ure, looping/over-action, unsafe trust of ex-ternal content, state/memory contamination, plus a valid no-failure label), each annotated with the two orthogonal hierarchical labels 

primary_error_source and impact .• Security — 258 items targeting tool-context isolation under poisoned tool outputs and related adversarial contexts (malicious doc-uments, hijacked tool responses, attacker-controlled email/web content). Each item is presented to the evaluator under 

two prompt modes :• Taxonomy-aware — the prompt names the closed-set label menu (the six control gates, or the nine trajectory categories, or the security action set) and asks the model to pick one with a short justification. • Taxonomy-blind — the prompt removes the label menu and asks the model to produce a free-form natural-language diagnosis. A de-6terministic substring rule then maps the free-form output back to the same closed set; un-matched outputs fall back to a single Haiku-4.5 mapper call (“which of these labels best fits this verdict?”). The dataset-generation pipeline (seed catalogue, prompt clauses, schema, dedupe, license audit, batch worker design) is described in Appendix B. 

7.2 Models and Metrics 

The eight evaluators are four frontier closed 

(Claude Sonnet 4.6, Claude Haiku 4.5, gpt-5.4-mini, Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite) and four open-weight (Qwen3.6-35B-A3B, Gemma-4-26B-A4B-it, Ministral-3-14B-Instruct-2512, gpt-oss-20B). Open models were served via local vLLM. Each (model × split × prompt-mode) cell is one run; the full sweep is 8 × 3 × 2 = 48 runs and ≈21,000 per-item judgments. Full per-(model × split ×

prompt-mode) tables are in Appendix C; a com-pact axis-by-axis reference is given by Table F.1 (Appendix F). The three headline findings are: • Control: aware-mode accuracy clusters within 7 pp across seven of eight models (0.87– 0.95); the explicit label menu alone makes models look similar. • Trajectory: removing the nine-category menu drops every model 14–40 pp ; the blind-mode floor compresses to 0.54–0.62 regard-less of family. • Tool-context utility: model rankings invert across axes — the model that tops control (Haiku 4.5) is the weakest frontier model on tool-context utility (0.28); the model that tops tool-context utility (gpt-5.4-mini) is the weak-est frontier model on trajectory. 

7.3 Results: Taxonomy Aware vs. Blind 

Under taxonomy-aware control prompting the eight models cluster within 7 pp (0.870–0.946 for the seven competitive models; gpt-oss-20B at 0.743 trails the pack). Trajectory aware accuracy spans an even narrower 0.69–0.95 range. Open Gemma-4-26B-A4B (0.943 trajectory aware) outranks fron-tier Sonnet 4.6 (0.885) and gpt-5.4-mini (0.818); Qwen3.6-35B-A3B trails Sonnet by under 5 pp on control. A single-number ranking on either taxonomy-aware split would not differentiate these eight models in any deployment-relevant way — exactly the §3 critique of OSWorld / WebArena rankings, replicated on our own data. 

Removing the explicit taxonomy from the prompt drops every model 14–40 pp on trajec-tory diagnosis (Fig. D.1 in Appendix D). Per-model drops range from −14.8 pp (gpt-oss-20B) to −40.1 pp (Gemma-4-26B-A4B), with frontier-vs-open family means within one point ( −30.5 vs 

−31.4 pp); the full per-model breakdown is in Ap-pendix C (Table C.2). The smallest drop is on an open-weight model and the largest is also on an open-weight model, so the contrast is not cleanly explained by a frontier-vs-open robustness gap. What does separate cleanly is the blind-mode floor :every model’s blind accuracy lands in an 8-point band (0.54–0.62), independent of whether it scored 0.69 or 0.95 in aware mode. Once the explicit la-bel menu is removed, the eight models become roughly indistinguishable on trajectory diagnosis — the strongest empirical support in the paper for the thesis that a single coarse-label aware-mode metric measures supervision more than capability. 

7.4 Results: Cross-Axis Incoherence No model wins all three axes (Fig. 4). Per-model radar profiles reveal sharp asymmetries that a tier-aggregated leaderboard hides. Haiku 4.5 dominates control (0.95) and trajectory (0.95) but is the weak-est frontier model on tool-context utility reten-tion (0.28) — it refuses 68% of benign tasks under aware mode and 84% under blind. gpt-5.4-mini inverts the pattern: it tops tool-context utility (0.98) but is mid-pack on control (0.91) and last among frontier on trajectory (0.82). Open Gemma-4-26B-A4B has the strongest maximin profile in the set 

— its worst axis is 0.87 (control), better than every other model’s worst-axis score (Sonnet 0.79, gpt-5.4-mini 0.82, Gemini 0.70, gpt-oss 0.69, Qwen 0.40, Haiku 0.28, Ministral 0.08). Gemma is not 

top-half on every individual axis (it sits 6th of 8 on control); it is the only model that avoids a sharp asymmetry across the three axes. The practical im-plication: a deployment team that picks by a single axis will end up with a model that is among the worst in the set on the axis they did not optimize. This replicates the τ -bench Pass 1-vs-Pass 4 rank flip within our own evaluation.

Several broad limitations apply to the synthesized landscape. Reported results from different pa-pers are not directly comparable because scaffolds, prompts, budgets, tools, and data conditions dif-7Figure 4: Per-model radar grid over (control accuracy, trajectory label accuracy, tool-context utility retention) under taxonomy-aware mode. Choosing by any single axis gives a model that is among the worst on another. 

fer; rapidly changing leaderboards may go stale, so the paper focuses on robust benchmark prop-erties rather than leaderboard-chasing; generated control-decision data may over-simplify produc-tion environments unless validated against real tool traces; security benchmarks are version-sensitive because MCP servers, clients, and provider poli-cies evolve quickly; and trajectory annotation is subjective unless labels are carefully defined and at least a subset is human-reviewed. In particu-lar, OSWorld’s public leaderboard mixes bare-model runs, tool-augmented scaffolds, and best-of-N voting; our quoted numbers do not nor-malize for inference-compute budget. Best-of-N submissions inflate the observed score with an order of magnitude more test-time compute than the same base model under single-shot — so leaderboard positions should not be read as pure-model rankings. 

Five further caveats apply specifically to the §7 empirical results. (i) Generator-locked. The 1,342-item dataset and all gold labels were pro-duced by a single Claude Opus 4.7 checkpoint, so “model X agrees with the gold” is conflated with “model X agrees with Opus’s preferences”; our headline aware-mode trajectory ordering plau-sibly reflects label-style overlap rather than diag-nostic capability. (ii) No human-validated cal-ibration subset. Absolute accuracy numbers in §7 are not directly comparable to HAL, MAST, or AgentRx, which anchor on human annota-tions. (iii) Small support in some Security cells 

(n = 3 refuse-gold, n = 7 stop-gold), so the security discussion in §7.4 leans on stable cells (benign_utility_retention at n = 192 act-gold, over_refusal_rate ) rather than per-label F1. (iv) Mapper accuracy assumed, not mea-sured. We estimate ≈3–5% of blind-mode predic-tions are routed to a wrong label by the substring + Haiku-fallback rule, so the 14–40 pp drop could be inflated by a few points; the cross-axis incoher-ence finding (Fig. 4) is unaffected because it uses aware-mode metrics only. (v) Step-boundary am-biguity. Critical-step exact accuracy (0.09–0.15) is treated as exploratory pending a definitional audit; the very large exact-vs-±1 gap (etiology in Ap-pendix B) is consistent with a labeling-convention mismatch rather than a uniform localization failure. The natural follow-ups — multi-generator regen, 100-item mapper audit, step-boundary audit — are the next required steps before §7 is treated as more than a measurement-protocol demonstration. 

9 Conclusion 

The next stage of agent evaluation should move be-yond final success rates. AgentAtlas does not pro-pose a new universal score; it offers a vocabulary for asking which agent behavior a score actually reflects. We unify fragmented benchmarks into a taxonomy of control decisions and trajectory fail-ures, audit fifteen benchmarks against six axes, and demonstrate on a fixed eight-model evaluation that 8prompt format and evaluation axis change apparent rankings. Outcome-only scores are increasingly insufficient and scaffold-sensitive: the central ques-tion is no longer just whether an agent succeeds, but whether it acts, asks, refuses, stops, confirms, and recovers at the right moments. The taxonomy com-plements rather than replaces existing benchmarks; future evaluations can use it as a checklist across tool choice, argument correctness, ambiguity, re-fusal calibration, stopping, recovery, state/memory, prompt injection, and cost/latency.

B Dataset Generation Pipeline 

The 1,342-item synthetic set summarized in §7.1 was built by a single-purpose pipeline that ex-ists alongside the eval harness. This appendix documents the generator, prompts, and validation passes. 

Generator and provider. All items and gold labels were produced by Claude Opus 4.7 via the Anthropic Messages API, using OIDC bearer-token auth with a project token to route against a project quota. The pipeline runs as a parallel batch dis-patcher (process pool, default concurrency 10 for Anthropic) with tenacity retry + JWT-exp token refresh per worker. 

Schema and prompts. Items are validated against per-split JSON schemas for Control, Tra-jectory, and Security. Each schema defines the closed-set gold label, structured context fields (user instruction, tools, state, ambiguity, hidden risk), and an Opus-validated explanation. Prompts in-ject the JSON schema into the system message, plus a per-cell seed catalogue: AST-extracted tool / argument / domain names from the τ -bench, Agent-Dojo, and ToolSandbox source code, sampled at runtime to avoid both repetition and license leak-age. 

Validation and dedupe. Generated items pass through two post-processing stages. First, a two-judge keep / revise / discard pass (Opus 4.7 gen-erator + a smaller verifier) flags items that are off-spec, leak benchmark text verbatim, or contain in-consistent gold labels. Second, a token-Jaccard near-duplicate filter removes items whose 5-gram overlap exceeds 0.7 with any earlier accepted item in the same split. After both passes, the final counts are 684 / 400 / 258 across Control / Trajectory / Security. 

Per-axis design intent. 

• Control — six gates × six domains, intention-ally weighted toward Confirm and Recover because evaluating irreversible actions is the hardest published gap. • Trajectory — nine categories from AgentRx plus the two orthogonal hierarchical labels 

primary_error_source and impact . Each category has at least 30 items so per-label F1 is reportable. • Security — items target tool-context iso-lation under poisoned tool outputs, mali-cious documents, and attacker-controlled email/web content. The gold action set ex-tends Control’s six gates with an explicit 

act-on-attack failure mode used to com-pute attack_following_rate .

Limitations. All items and labels are produced by one model family; this is the dominant caveat (§8, caveat (i)). We did not collect a human-validated calibration subset (caveat (ii)). The map-per that converts taxonomy-blind free-form outputs back to closed-set labels is a deterministic substring rule with a Haiku-4.5 fallback for unmatched cases; we estimate ≈3–5% of blind outputs get routed to a wrong label (caveat (iv)).
